FAQ

What are paper miniatures?

Miniature figurines have a long history in games and storytelling. Think of chess pieces and ceramic dolls, hand puppets and marionettes. By bringing a physical item into existence, we can hold a small part of the story we're telling in our hands.

Paper miniatures are an easy way to make those little physical representations of the characters and creatures in our stories. They're commonly associated with tabletop role-playing games, like Dungeons & Dragons.

You can use paper miniatures however you like. You could make your own paper chess set, prototype a new board game, or print some little characters from whatever book you're reading as a bedtime story. Have fun!

How can I print an SVG?

You'll need a tool that lets you paste an SVG from your clipboard, and arrange it on a page, and export the page as a PDF. You can usually fit many different miniatures on a single page.

Figma is likely the easiest option if you're new to vector editing tools. Inkscape is another option, it's a free and open-source vector graphics editor but might have a bit more of a learning curve.

How do I assemble a paper mini?

You'll need scissors and a glue stick.

First cut along the solid lines. you don’t have to be super exact, a little variation is fine. Then fold along the dotted lines.

Once you’re done each individual fold, fold everything into place to see how your paper miniature will look. Finally, glue along the entire back of the print to permanently assemble your miniature.

What kinds of images can I use?

Please source your images responsibly. Most image formats should work well. JPEG and PNG are likely best.

Images should have a white or very light background. This is helpful for tracing, and also prints nicely. If you need a way to remove a background, https://www.remove.bg/ works well, the small image sizes you get for free are fine for miniature sized printing.

Images should be under 1000 pixels square, otherwise things might be really slow. If you need an online image resizing tool, many have annoying ads, but Adobe Express's resize tool can be refreshing because it's just one giant ad for Adobe.

If your image isn’t working as expected, you can try to tweak the silhouette related settings to see if that helps.

Should I use AI-generated images?

Using generative algorithms to create character artwork is a shortcut. I think it's important to consider what you’re cutting out, and the nature of the tools you’re using.

By their nature, generative algorithms are derivative of existing images. To train these algorithms, AI companies download and process, without permission, the creative work of many generations of hard-working artists. I think it's critically important to acknowledge the lifetimes of human labour that underpin these tools. The companies that build and sell these tools did not create the words and images that power them. These companies seem happy to compromise the livelihoods of the same people to whom they owe their existence, all in the name of profit and financial progress.

When you use generative algorithms, you are cutting out the artist. And this is just one issue of many related to generative algorithms. It’s up to you to source and create images responsibly.

You may be cutting something else out too. The value proposition of generative algorithms is that they let you skip a good chunk of the creative process. You may find that hand-drawing your own art brings a different kind of connection and meaning to what you create. No matter how “bad” you might think you are as an artist, there’s no way to know what you’re missing until you’ve personally created something you feel happy with.

I strongly believe that feeling happy with your own artwork doesn’t mean it needs to be “good” by any standard. I think that desire for “good” artwork, and protection from the feeling that we’re “bad” at something, is what drives us to use sloppy and exploitative generative tools in the first place.

If you focus on a playful and forgiving creative process, your joy will be baked into whatever you make, even if it doesn’t have as much lipstick as what gets spit out of an image generator.

How can I support this project?

If you've gotten something out of the many hours of work that went into this tool, you can say thanks with money. It'll help me buy food and pay my rent.

If you'd like to contribute code or feedback, you can do so on GitHub at zchsh/paper-mini-gen.

The spirit of this project is to make a tool that's open source, respects user data by collecting nothing, does one thing really well, and uses file formats that are highly interoperable, so that you can use it in combination with other tools. It's an attempt at taking a Unix-like philosophy to writing code for the web.

If you like writing code, I'd love to hear your thoughts or collaborate on tools with a similar philosophy. You can reach me at hi@zch.sh.

Colophon & thanks

This project relies on a bunch of open source libraries. A big thanks to maintainers of these libraries for making their work freely available.

  • Jimp - used for image processing
  • ImageTracerJS - used for image tracing
  • FlattenSVG - used to simplify traced vectors
  • Javascript Clipper - used for outlining and boolean operations

This project is open source. You can view the source code on GitHub at zchsh/paper-mini-gen.
